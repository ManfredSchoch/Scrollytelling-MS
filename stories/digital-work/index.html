A research story · Manfred Schoch

Every login, message, and click at work leaves a digital trace.

Behind those traces are people — and no two adopt technology the same way.

The real test isn't how fast we roll out tools — it's how we bring the whole workforce along.

Scroll ↓

Bringing new technology into an organization is rarely just a technical problem — it is a human one. My research reads the digital traces people leave to understand how different groups actually adopt, struggle with, and adapt to digital work — from everyday workplace tools to AI agents — and what organizations can do to bring everyone along.

Act 1 · How people actually use technology

No two people use tech the same

Using digital trace data — the anonymized logs of everyday tool use — we studied how different types of employees used digital communication and collaboration over time. People don't move in lockstep: some are intensive power users, others adopt slowly, and a sudden external shock can push each group in a different direction.

Same shock, opposite moves

Between two observation periods, an external shock hit — the sudden shift to remote work. It didn't move everyone the same way.

Groups that had used few digital channels — much of the administrative staff — broadened their tool use. Groups that were already intensive users — parts of middle management — narrowed the variety of channels they relied on. New situations reshape habits, but not uniformly.

Zoom out, and the same pattern appears across many studies and many different triggers: technology use is never simply “rolled out and done.” It shifts over time — but only when something prompts it. Each adaptation differs in length and intensity, and then settles into a new steady state.

New situations are openings — moments where organizations can actively reshape how technology is used.

Act 2 · The human cost

What digital work costs

The same connectivity that makes us productive can quietly wear people down. Two threads of our research make this concrete: how a constant stream of IT demands adds up to a felt lack of time — and how people's emotions toward technology shift as they get used to it.

Dozens of small frictions — interruptions, notifications, ever-changing tools — rarely register on their own. Together they converge into something people feel acutely: never enough time. And that scarcity radiates outward.

It doesn't only wear people down

Adopting technology isn't only strain. Following the same users over time, two emotions move in opposite directions: the sense of challenge fades as people find their footing, while a sense of achievement grows. And challenge is productive — it is what drives people to adapt how they work.

From challenge to mastery

Early on, new technology feels like a challenge. Over time that feeling recedes, and a sense of achievement takes its place.

The challenge phase isn't wasted: it is precisely what pushes people to adapt how they use the technology. Give people the room to get there, and strain turns into competence.

Give people time to find their footing, and strain turns into mastery.

Act 3 · Designing the support

Meeting people where they get stuck

Knowing what digital work does to people is only useful if it changes how we design their support. Three complementary moves: faster help when self-coping stalls, in-the-moment nudges, and building people's own coping skills.

A second move meets people in the moment. We designed a personalized support app that turns the very source of the problem — notifications — into the channel for helping people regain control of their attention: it surfaces one small, actionable tip at a time, keeps a history, and lets people reflect on their own interruption load.

From lab to practice

Redesigning the front line of support

These ideas shape real projects. With a university IT service desk and the central student-advisory office, we are rethinking how support works: routine, automatable requests — the recurring FAQs — are handled by AI, so human advisors can concentrate on the complex cases and the moments where personal contact really matters.

Bringing the strategy along

Technology only reaches the whole workforce when leadership and strategy come with it. In applied projects and executive training — including a change-management and new-digital-work strategy programme with a large organization — we help leaders shape how digital work is introduced, not just which tools are bought. Our Fraunhofer study New Digital Work distils much of this guidance for practitioners.
