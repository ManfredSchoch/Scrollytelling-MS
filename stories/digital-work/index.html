A research story · Manfred Schoch

Every login, message, and click at work leaves a digital trace.

Behind those traces are people — and no two adopt technology the same way.

The real test isn't how fast we roll out AI — it's how we bring the whole workforce along.

Scroll ↓

Every wave of new technology raises the same question — and AI raises it louder than ever: not how fast can we roll it out? but how do we bring our people with us? My research starts from the human side of digital work — how different people actually adopt new tools, what the experience does to them, and how organizations can support them. One lesson runs through all of it: lasting change is evolution, not revolution.

Act 1 · Adoption is a journey

No two people adopt at the same pace

New technology never lands in an organization all at once. Using digital trace data — the anonymized logs of everyday tool use — we watched different groups adopt digital tools over time. People don't move in lockstep: some race ahead, others take longer, and a single external event can push each group in a different direction.

Same shock, opposite moves

Between two observation periods, an external shock hit — the sudden shift to remote work. It didn't move everyone the same way.

Groups that had used few digital channels — much of the administrative staff — broadened their tool use. Groups that were already intensive users — parts of middle management — narrowed the variety of channels they relied on. New situations reshape habits, but not uniformly.

The same story is now unfolding with AI. Access is no longer the differentiator — almost everyone has the tools. Adoption is: a small group of enthusiasts already drives most of the AI activity, while many colleagues are still finding their footing. It echoes a pattern we first saw in workplace data years ago — a handful of power users account for the lion's share of what happens.

Zoom out, and the same rhythm appears across many studies and many different triggers: technology use is never simply “rolled out and done.” It shifts over time — but only when something prompts it. Each adaptation differs in length and intensity, and then settles into a new steady state.

Lasting change is evolution, not revolution — you can't skip the steps, but you can shape them.

Which is why one-size-fits-all rollouts leave most people behind. If everyone starts from a different place, the support has to meet them there — tailored to the individual. That is where our practice begins (see below).

Act 2 · The human experience

From friction to mastery

Adopting something new — now, above all, AI — rarely feels effortless at first. It can feel like a challenge, even a threat. But follow the same people over time, and something hopeful happens.

Challenge becomes achievement

Early on, a new tool — a new AI assistant — feels like a challenge. Over time that feeling recedes, and a sense of achievement takes its place.

The challenge phase isn't wasted: it is precisely what pushes people to adapt how they work. Give people the room to get there, and early strain turns into real competence.

The early struggle with AI isn't failure — it's the first step of mastery.

Act 3 · Human-first support

Don't leave people stuck

Evolution still needs a hand. When people hit a wall — a tool that won't cooperate, an AI answer they can't judge — and try to push through alone, the clock runs and patience drains. Human-first support means noticing the struggle early and offering a fast, low-friction path to help.

And beyond fixing today's problem, human-first support helps people build their own coping skills — so the next hurdle is smaller. In a behavioural-change experiment, a short course taught people concrete strategies for handling IT demands: the goal is not just a working tool, but a more capable, more confident user.

From lab to practice

Bringing AI to people, in practice

Because people adopt at different paces, we don't hand everyone the same slide deck. We run AI trainings tailored to the individual — meeting each role and skill level where it is, turning hesitant first contact into confident everyday use. It is the human-first idea from Act 1, put to work: no one is left behind.

Designing an AI-supported help desk

The same thinking reshapes support itself — and here it is not just a concept but a running system. With a university IT service desk and the central student-advisory office, we automate the routine part of the ticketing process end to end: incoming tickets are classified, and the recurring, automatable ones — the FAQs — are answered by AI, while human advisors are freed for the complex cases and the moments where personal contact really matters.

Bringing the strategy along

Technology only reaches the whole workforce when leadership and strategy come with it. In applied projects and executive training — including a change-management and new-digital-work strategy programme with a large organization — we help leaders shape how digital work and AI are introduced, not just which tools are bought. Our Fraunhofer study New Digital Work distils much of this guidance for practitioners.
